“charlesʼs wain” (MS III; Poems [1891]; Works [1903])—Noting the reference to Charlesʼs Wain, <a href="/notes/viljoen_h_g_note" target="_self">Helen Gill Viljoen</a> (<a href="/notes/bibliography#VILJOENPAPERS" target="_self">“Viljoen Papers”</a>, box F.X) remarks that Ruskin could have learned the constellations from <a href="/notes/joyce_jeremiah" target="_self">Jeremiah Joyce</a> (<a href="/notes/bibliography#JOYCESCIENTIFICDIALOGUES" target="_self">Joyce, Scientific Dialogues</a>, ) or from <a href="/notes/day_thomas_note" target="_self">Thomas Day</a> (<a href="/notes/bibliography#DAYSANDFORDANDMERTON" target="_self">Day, Sandford and Merton</a>, ). For Ruskinʼs interest in this constellation, see also “Harry and Lucy”, Vol. 2, chap. 1; and “The Constellations: Northern, Some of the Zodiac, and Some of the Southern”.


Between lines 24 and 25—The Library Edition incorrectly identifies John James Ruskin as adding the date 9 September 1826. The hand, which is certainly Margaret Ruskinʼs, is identical to that for a similar annotation in MS I.


“[lost]” (Poems [1891]; Works [1903])—<a href="/notes/collingwood_w_g_note" target="_self">W. G. Collingwood</a> comments that the “word in square brackets”, which he supplied, “is wanting in the original” (<a href="/notes/bibliography#JRPOEMS4D1891" target="_self">Poems [4o, 1891]</a>, 1:xxv; <a href="/notes/bibliography#JRPOEMS8D1891" target="_self">Poems [8o, 1891]</a>, 1:xii). In the sole extant manuscript version, there is no evidence of such a word existing. Collingwood assumes Ruskinʼs intention, since, without the word, the second line in the stanza would deviate from the “abab” rhyme scheme that prevails throughout the remainder of poem.
