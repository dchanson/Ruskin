Title
In the presentation copy, now bound in MS XI, Ruskin wrote the title as “THE SUN”; and in MS III, he wrote the title in capitals without emphasis, as “THE SUN”.
See System of Title Citation for Works.
Genre
New Year’s poem.
Manuscripts
MS XI; MS III.
In MS XI, “The Sun” is printed by Ruskin in pencil as a presentation copy for New Yearʼs 1828, followed on the same sheet by “Glenfarg” and “Glen of Glenfarg” (“Papa how pretty those icicles are”).
In MS III, which is a Red Book devoted initially to “Harry and Lucy . . . Vol 2, “The Sun” appears as the third poem in the MS III Second Poetry Anthology. Here, following the conclusion of the poem, Ruskin prominently inscribed the date “January 1 1828”; and the poem is followed by a copy of “Glenfarg” and “Glen of Glenfarg” (“Papa how pretty those icicles are”), thus repeating the sequence of those two poems as printed in the presentation copy (MS XI).

Date
1 January 1828.
Dated by Ruskin both in the presentation copy (MS XI) and in the Red Book (MS III).
See System of Date Citation.
Composition & Publication
Published with omissions in Poems (4o, 1891), 1:5–6; and Poems (8o, 1891), 1:5–6; and in full in Ruskin, Works, 2:260–61.
As part of the MS III Second Poetry Anthology in MS III, “The Sun” forms part of a cluster of New Yearʼs poems, including the poem paired with it in the holiday presentation for New Yearʼs 1828, “Glenfarg” and “Glen of Glenfarg” (“Papa how pretty those icicles are”), and the poem for New Yearʼs 1827, “Time: Blank Verse”. As an overlapping cluster, the anthology begins with a poem related to “The Sun” thematically, as a poem about astronomy, “The Constellations” (see Discussion).
Discussion
Sources
In the first half of “THE SUN”, before swerving to its occasion as a holiday greeting, the poem is a hymn of praise. Specifically, with its image of “mamma beside me” walking in the garden, Ruskinʼs praise of the sun condenses Adam and Eveʼs morning hymn in book 5 of Paradise Lost, “These are thy glorious works, Parent of good”. Ruskin relies on Miltonʼs organizing trope of the sunʼs course from dawn to noon to sunset, and he draws on some of Miltonʼs diction, such as invoking the sun to “disperse” the night:
Thou Sun, of this great World both Eye and Soul,
Acknowledge him thy Greater, sound his praise
In thy eternal course, both when thou climbʼst,
And when high Noon hast gainʼd, and when thou fallʼst.
Moon, that now meetʼst the orient Sun, now fliʼst
With the fixt Stars, fixt in thir Orb that flies,
And yee five other wandʼring Fires that move
In mystic Dance not without Song, resound
His praise, who out of Darkness callʼd up Light.
Air, and ye Elements the eldest birth
Of Natureʼs Womb, that in quaternion run
Perpetual Circle, multiform, and mix
And nourish all things, let your ceaseless change
Vary to our great Maker still new praise.
Ye Mists and Exhalations that now rise
From Hill or steaming Lake, dusky or gray,
Till the Sun paint your fleecy skirts with Gold,
In honor to the Worldʼs great Author rise,
Whether to deck with Clouds thʼ uncolorʼd sky,
Or wet the thirsty Earth with falling showers,
Rising or falling still advance his praise.
His praise ye Winds, that from four Quarters blow,
Breathe soft or loud; and wave your tops, ye Pines,
With every Plant, in sign of Worship wave.
Fountains and yee, that warble, as ye flow,
Melodious murmurs, warbling tune his praise.
Join voices all ye living Souls; ye Birds,
That singing up to Heaven Gate ascend,
Bear on your wings and in your notes his praise;
Yee that in Waters glide, and yee that walk
The Earth, and stately tread, or lowly creep;
Witness if I be silent, Morn or Even,
To Hill, or Valley, Fountain, or fresh shade
Made vocal by my Song, and taught his praise.
Hail universal Lord, be bounteous still
To give us only good; and if the night
Have gatherʼd aught of evil or concealʼd,
Disperse it, as now light dispels the dark.
(Milton, Paradise Lost, bk. 5, lines 171–208, in Complete Poems and Major Prose, ed. Hughes, 306–7)
In MS III, the astronomical theme forming the conclusion of “The Sun” shares an interest in constellations with other works in MS III—part of the narrative in “Harry and Lucy . . . Vol 2”, and the poems “The Constellations: Northern, Some of the Zodiac, and Some of the Southern” and “Glen of Glenfarg” (“Glen of Glenfarg thy beauteous rill”).
