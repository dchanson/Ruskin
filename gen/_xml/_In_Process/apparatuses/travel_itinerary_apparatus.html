Title
Untitled by Ruskin.
Genre
Imitation of a travel guide.
Manuscripts
MS IVB.
Date
Before May and through mid‐July 1828. The two lists of itineraries (see Composition and Publication) can be dated only approximately, owing to the failure of any family letters to survive from 1828, except for a New Yearʼs address dated from the last day of the year (RFL 170‐71). One letter ascribed to earlier in 1828, a poem for John Jamesʼs birthday, is an error in RFL (see no. 41); therefore, we cannot be certain that the family departed Herne Hill following John Jamesʼs May 10 birthday. Some dates in May through July can be attached to the actual tour. The prospective list would have been compiled prior to departure, of course, presumably at a time when John James was at home to help annotate the list.
Composition and Publication
Portion reproduced in Hunt, John Ruskin: A Wider Sea, fig. 6a. Quoted in Ruskin, Works, 1:xxv‐xxvi n. 2. Previously unpublished in entirety.
See Discussion for the genesis of the travel plans. MS IVB contains two itineraries, one at the front of the notebook, and the other at the back, consisting of lists of city names, sometimes annotated with sightseeing features to be found in those towns and cities.
Helen Viljoen interpreted the itinerary at the front of the Red Book as Ruskinʼs prospective plan with annotations by John James, and the list at the end as notes taken during the actual trip (“Dating MSS. of Boyhood,” HGVP, boxes D.V, E.IX). Viljoenʼs interpretation is supported by the prospective list being printed neatly in ink by Ruskin (longhand annotations by John James), while the notes for the actual tour are more roughly written in Ruskinʼs ink longhand.
Thus, it appears, sometime prior to departure, Ruskin devised the list of prospective places by dividing pages in half horizontally, and labeling each half‐page with a place name. Most sections were then annotated by John James with features of special interest, obviously drawing on some guidebook, and, one imagines, consulting with his wife and son.
Later, during the journey itself, Ruskin listed merely the names of places they visited, the swing back to London occurring soon after arriving in Plymouth, where the family learned of Aunt Jessieʼs death.
Some years later, the lists became densely crowded by Ruskinʼs mineralogy notes, written reverso from the other end of the notebook (see no. 171; and part 2, MS IVB, “Content”).
Discussion
These travel itineraries and notes record plans for the Ruskinsʼ “great tour” of the Lake District in 1828. The tour was planned to compensate for the leaner years of 1826 and 1827, when business kept John James Ruskin on the road traveling for orders longer into the spring than normal.
As it turned out, the tour to the Lakes was not realized until 1830, the 1828 journey having been interrupted on 24 May by news of the 12 May death of “Aunt Jessie,” John Jamesʼs sister, Mrs. Patrick Richardson (see Tour to the Lakes in Cumberland, ed. Burd and Dearden, 7; and Ruskin, Works, 35:70, where the death is mistakenly ascribed to 1829).
For another instance of Ruskinʼs preplanning trips, see RFL, 220.
