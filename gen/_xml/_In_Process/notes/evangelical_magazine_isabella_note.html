The Ruskins and The Evangelical Magazine
In a letter of 3 March 1823 to John James Ruskin, Margaret Ruskin mentions receiving this publication (Ruskin Family Letters, ed. Burd, 121). Her remark does not make clear when the Ruskinsʼ subscription began, but it might be significant that, with the 1823 issues, the magazine initiated volume 1 of a new series.
The Evangelical Magazine was founded in 1793 as a nondenominational voice of evangelical culture embracing Anglican Evangelicalism, Old Dissent, and New Dissent (Altholz, Religious Press in Britain, 45). That spirit of pan‐evangelical catholicity of opinion and mission was reaffirmed at the start of the new series in 1823, but the position was being tested. A “Retrospect” published in the first issue of the new series admitted to the emergence of rival denominational publications on both the Anglican Evangelical and Congregational sides. In fact, the editorship of the Evangelical Magazine itself became dominated by Congregationalists, just as that sect prevailed within the organization that the magazine most prominently represented, the London Missionary Society (LMS) (“Retrospect of the Former Series of the Evangelical Magazine”, 1; Altholz,Religious Press in Britain, 46).
So much of each issue was given over to news and formal reports of LMS activity that the Ruskinsʼ (or at least Margaretʼs) regular reading of the Evangelical Magazine implies a strong interest in missionary work. The reports included lists of charitable contributions to the LMS, which, as Sujit Sivasundaram argues, inspired readers with a collective experience of evangelical zeal. The lists comprised both large and small donations, even so modest as used copies of the to be sent to missionaries abroad, who duly reported back with their gratitude for fresh reading to relieve the tedium of their missions. Readers therefore felt they played a part in a worldwide evangelistic effort (“The Periodical as Barometer”, 43-48).
Such outreach was characteristic of the LMSʼs larger effort in print culture, which Anna Johnston describes as the construction of "a reading audience through its canny manipulation of textual and media technologies, a readership that would unite religious Britons in a common cause despite differences of class, region, gender, and age" (, 30). The young John Ruskin was captured by the excitement, as evidenced by , which appears to record a speech or sermon at a meeting of a missionary society. Ruskin was also vulnerable to the appeal of missionary celebrity, as shown by his starstruck enthusiasm for the poetry and journals of Reginald Heber, Bishop of Calcutta.
Besides reporting on the missionary activities of LMS, the Evangelical Magazine addressed other interests that probably engaged the Ruskin family. Each issue contained a section of poetry, albeit strictly pious in subject matter; and the January issue routinely printed poems for the New Year, which may have influenced Ruskin's custom of composing a New Year's ode to be fair-copied and presented to his father on New Year's Eve. Feature articles mainly concerned religious topics but occasionally encompassed other areas of knowledge, such as natural history. A section of book reviews and brief notices of religious publications, including pious works for children, evidently informed the elder Ruskins' choice of some books for John, since some notices correspond to books that he is known to have owned (see, e.g., . Among the most prominent features in each issue were lengthy obituaries and biographies of celebrated clergy, embellished by an engraved portrait of a clergyman, which supplied the sole graphic interest in each issue, apart from the engraved banner. These biographies exposed the reader to peoples, landscapes, and customs of cultures around the world, albeit filtered through the perception of missionaries.
