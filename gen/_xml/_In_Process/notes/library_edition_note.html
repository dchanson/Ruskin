Library Edition

Edited by E. T. Cook and Alexander Wedderburn, and published 1903–12 by George Allen, this 39-volume set was the first collected edition of Ruskinʼs works to attempt comprehensiveness. Allen himself oversaw production and release of several volumes before his death in 1907.
The Library Edition presented a significant departure from the first collected edition, the Works Series, which Ruskin dramatically limited in scope. The new edition aimed at collecting all of the published works, along with a considerable extent of unpublished materials such as drafts, letters, and diary extracts.
Despite these aims of comprehensiveness, the results for the early works were mixed. The first two volumes containing Early Prose (vol. 1 ) and Poems (vol. 2 [1903]) were hurried along by Allen, who was anxious to prevent subscribers from perceiving default of the edition. He thus caused the editors, Wedderburn and Cook, to rely heavily on Allenʼs previously published editions of early works and, especially in the case of the early prose, to cobble together compilations of the earlier “editing.” The volume of Poems, too, shows the seams between merely reprinting W. G. Collingwoodʼs work on the Poems of 1891 and attempting to deliver on the editionʼs promises by expanding on that earlier editorial work.
Motivations for the unprecedented comprehensiveness of the edition were both financial and philosophical. Wedderburn, as one of Ruskinʼs three literary trustees, urged as early as March, 1900, following Ruskinʼs death in January, that copyrights of Ruskinʼs published books be extended by inclusion of new material. This would profit himself, who stood to gain by terms of Ruskinʼs will, and George Allen, who as publisher gained by terms of his former agreement with Ruskin. Of the two other trustees, Joan Severn and Charles Eliot Norton, the former looked forward to profit while the latter dissented, expressing a fundamentally different editorial philosophy from Wedderburnʼs. For Norton, guided by Ruskinʼs procedure in the Works Series, the editorʼs responsibility was to omit all materials except those he judged important and even to destroy manuscript materials that might prove embarrassing to a writerʼs reputation.
W. G. Collingwood, who as editor of Poems (1891) had demonstrated sound editorial abilities, but who, unlike Wedderburn, gained nothing as an editor by terms of Ruskinʼs will, repudiated what he saw as Wedderburnʼs and the Severnsʼ greed, while agreeing with Wedderburn in editorial philosophy. This left Wedderburn, Allen, and Joan Severn as collaborators on the new edition, who projected a profit of £30,000 to be split as even thirds.
Norton remained critical of what he saw as “gripe– all –characters” in Wedderburn and Allen (quoted in Dearden, “Library Edition of John Ruskin,” 53), but he tended to exempt Joan Severn from greed, (because she was a woman?) For Allen, surely, the project represented not only new profits but also an opportunity for fine bookmaking. His first endeavor was to commission handmade paper watermarked with Ruskinʼs monogram and coat of arms, designed by W. H. Hooper ().
With Collingwood distancing himself from the scheme, Wedderburn required a collaborator with editorial experience, whom he found in E. T. Cook. Although Cook was responsible for the greater part of editorial labor, Wedderburn always treated his coeditor with condescension, paying him £1,500 for ten years of scholarship.
Cook worked from typed and longhand transcripts, bound in red cloth (now at Bodleian and RL), made by .
According to the “Publisherʼs Notice” announcing completion of the edition (where is this?) , 2,062 sets were produced, with 2,000 for sale. Each volume in the standard maroon Holliston cloth cost 1 guinea (more for special bindings), which subscribers could pay on receipt of each volume, although separate volumes were not for sale apart from a set. By 1912, on completion of the series, sales had slowed sufficiently that remaining unbound sheets were sold to Encyclopedia Britannica, which issued sets in blue cloth along with the special incentive of a separately bound Ruskin letter (supplied by the Allen family). When George Allen & Company was reorganized as George Allen & Unwin in 1914, remaining bound copies passed to the new company, which continued to sell them.
