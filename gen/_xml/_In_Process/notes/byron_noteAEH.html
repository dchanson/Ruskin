Byron

George Gordon, Lord Byron (1788–1824)

In January 1834, when Ruskinʼs father wrote in reply to James Hogg (ca. 1770–1835), who had asked for an account of Ruskinʼs progress in using his wonderful abilities, John James included a sample of Johnʼs rhymes, possibly taken from Account of a Tour on the Continent, which Ruskin was composing at that time. Hoggʼs critique of the lines, which is known only from Ruskinʼs reply to Hogg, dwelled on the boyʼs imitation of Scott and Byron, presumably intending to encourage greater originality. Ruskinʼs comment identifies the boyʼs sense of how these two poets related to one another: “I have read Byron with wonder, and Scott with delight, they have caused me many a day-dream and night-dream” (Garden, ed., Memorials of James Hogg, 277).
