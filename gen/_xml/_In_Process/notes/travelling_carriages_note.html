Travelling Carriages

The Ruskins' Traveling Carriages

Henry Telford's Post Chaise
Readers of Praeterita are familiar with Ruskin's description of the "trevelling chariot" used for the family's summer holidays, which was lent by John James's business partner, Henry Telford (d. 1859), in the years of Ruskin's youth, "before my father thought it right to hire a carriage" for long journeys (Ruskin, Works, 35:29). In documentary evidence related to the Ruskins' 1827 journey to Scotland, John James lists a small expense connected with “chaise Perth”--presumably, the Telford carriage driven to Perth, since the terms post chaise, posting chariot, and travelling chariot describe the same type of vehicle, as remarked in English Pleasure Carriages (1837) (John James Ruskin, Account Book [1827–45], 2r; 225 see Tours of 1826–27: Wales and Scotland, 1827).
Surviving examples of this kind of carriage from the early nineteenth century can be viewed at the National Trust Carriage Museum at Arlington. As the museum's guidebook explains, the term chariot refers to the "shape of the body", seating only two passengers--or as Ruskin comments in Praeterita, "two persons and so much of third personage as I possessed at three years old"--on a "seat behind the doors", as opposed to the design of a coach, which seated "four inside the body" on "a seat ahead of the doors and one behind them". Ruskin's seat as a small boy "was the little box containing my clothes, strongly made, with a cushion on one end of it", which, "set upright in front (and well forward)", afforded him a "horizon of sight the widest possible" through the large front windows and the adjacent side windows on the doors. While Ruskin says he "was thus not the least in . . . [his parents'] way", his box would have obstructed a set of two folding panels that--if so equipped, and as explained by the Carriage Museum, could "be let down" from the wall beaneath the windows, enabling "the inside passengers to stretch their legs at full length into the boot and sleep while they travelled", although John's box could perhaps have been shifted to one side to let down one of the two panels (Nicholson, Parker‐Williams, and Martin, The National Trust Carriage Museum at Arlington Court Guidebook, 4; Ruskin, Works, 35:29, 32; “Conservation at the Carriage Museum”).
