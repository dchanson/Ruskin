Daily Routines and Schedules

Daily Routines and Schedules at Herne Hill and on Family Travels

Daily routine in the Ruskin household or during family travels can elude recovery, since rituals tend to be remarked only in the breach, and in any case likely changed over time. Some evidence of Ruskinʼs daily life in boyhood can be assembled, however, and perhaps contextualized.
Lessons and Meals
In his “Harry and Lucy Concluded” narratives, Ruskin repeatedly records times for “Harryʼs” lessons and play periods in relation to the meals of dinner and tea. According to “Harry and Lucy . . . Vol I”, Harry “always went to his lessons at twelve” (chap. 9) at least when vacationing at Hastings, having spent the morning playing on the beach or taking a walk with his father. Noon lessons preceded the midday meal of dinner, which occurred around 3:00 p.m. This time is evidenced by Harryʼs disorientation on the longest day of the year, the summer soltice, when the lingering light caused evening tea to arrive “seemingly at three o‐clock”—the dinner time—instead of its proper time of 7:00 p.m. (chap. 8). Similarly, according to “Harry and Lucy . . . Vol 2”, a disorientation hinges on this mealtime, when “[a]fter dinner” he “had to learn his lessons” again because they had been “quite driven them out of his head. But he learned them so quickly that he had from three to nine to play but he did not play he spent from three to half past it in learning his lessons for tomorrow and spent the rest in drawing took tea and went to bed” (chap. 1).
Dinner at three in the afternoon, as Judith Flanders shows, was considered an older convention in the nineteenth‐century—observed, for example, by Dora Spenlowʼs old‐fashioned maiden aunts in David Copperfield, who correspondingly take their tea at six‐thirty in the evening. Fashionable life later in the century called for evening dining at six or seven. Even the elder Ruskins, when entertaining, liked an evening dinner, so John James could sit up until midnight talking with friends. When married, John and Effie apparently set an unusually early dinner of one in the afternoon at their house in Park Street, London, followed by an evening tea. This meal schedule perhaps revolved around Johnʼs work, just as meals were timed with his lessons in boyhood (Flanders, Inside the Victorian Home, 267–68; Dickens, David Copperfield, ed. Burgis, 584 [chap. 41]; Cooper, Effie, 55).
